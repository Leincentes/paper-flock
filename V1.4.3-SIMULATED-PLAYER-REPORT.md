# Paper Flock v1.4.4 — Simulated Player Study

## Evidence status

**This is AI-simulated customer testing, not real-customer evidence.** The mechanical results use the actual Paper Flock campaign boards and rules. Persona reactions are design hypotheses that require observation with real players.

## Scope

- Build examined: Paper Flock v1.4.4 candidate
- Campaign: 40 actual boards
- Mechanical model: exact recursive probability over each reachable board state
- Strategies: random legal move, maximize immediate rotations, and one-step deadlock avoidance
- Recovery model: up to two independent restarts
- Simulated customer panel: 30 fictional but segment-based players
- Sound policy tested: enabled by default for new/reset profiles; explicit existing preferences preserved

## How the game is likely to be played

1. A new player sees the five-step tutorial and learns clear paths, blockers, clockwise neighbor rotation, and Undo.
2. The first three campaign levels are forced or nearly forced, creating quick early wins.
3. Level 4 intentionally introduces a tempting dead end and expects the player to recover with Undo.
4. Levels 5–9 widen the decision space while remaining forgiving.
5. Levels 10–11 form the first meaningful difficulty wall. Level 11 is especially deceptive because a locally attractive move can create a later deadlock.
6. Players who understand Undo, inspect rotations, or use hints can continue; impulsive players are more likely to restart repeatedly or leave.
7. The Journal, feathers, themes, and next-goal recommendation support completion-oriented replay after the core mechanic is understood.

|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| Level 1 | 100.0% | 100.0% | 100.0% | Immediate, reliable first win |
| Level 4 | 50.0% | 87.5% | 100.0% | Deliberate Undo lesson |
| Level 10 | 50.0% | 87.5% | 100.0% | First clear planning test |
| Level 11 | 48.8% | 86.6% | 58.3% | Strongest early heuristic trap |
| Levels 1–10 cumulatively | 11.1% | 74.2% | 100.0% | Recovery skill changes retention sharply |
| Level 20 | 24.0% | 56.1% | 81.2% | Chapter-one mastery gate |
| Level 21 | 100.0% | 100.0% | 100.0% | Good chapter-two difficulty reset |
| Level 30 | 27.9% | 62.5% | 100.0% | Second major trap |

These probabilities describe the modeled strategy, not human completion rates. Human players can Undo, seek hints, learn from animation, or abandon a level.

## Simulated panel summary

| Segment | Players | Simulated reaction | Main possible risk |
|---|---:|---|---|
| Casual relaxers | 6 | Strong visual first impression and likely tutorial completion | Repeated deadlocks around Levels 10–11 may feel unexplained |
| Puzzle enthusiasts | 5 | Likely to value the rotation depth and mastery layer | Level 11 may feel arbitrary if the causal lesson is unclear |
| Short-session commuters | 5 | Quick boards and checkpoint resume fit the context | Sound in public and hard-to-find mute controls can create immediate friction |
| Low-confidence or older newcomers | 4 | Calm visuals and Undo are reassuring | Rotation consequences may be visually subtle |
| Sound or motion-sensitive players | 4 | Settings and reduced-motion support build trust | Sound-on default must be paired with immediate, persistent control |
| Completionists | 3 | Journal, achievements, and feathers are strong motivators | Save reliability is non-negotiable |
| Low-end and offline-first players | 3 | Offline installation is a major benefit | Update and cache failures would cause abandonment |

## Sound-default decision

### Implemented

- New profiles start with sound enabled.
- A confirmed full-progress reset returns sound to enabled.
- Existing saves with `soundEnabled: false` remain off.
- Audio still begins only after a user interaction, respecting browser autoplay restrictions.
- The setting remains available in the Game section of Settings.

### Possible risk

Players in public spaces or with sensory sensitivity may hear an unexpected first effect before locating Settings. This is a design risk, not a verified complaint.

## Best recommendation

### Add an adaptive Level 11 recovery lesson before adding more content

After the player's first deadlock on Level 11, show a one-time, non-penalizing strategy message:

> More open paths are not always safer. Undo the last move and watch which neighboring bird turns.

Then visually emphasize **Undo** and briefly highlight the rotated neighbor. Do not automatically solve the move and do not deduct an additional feather beyond the deadlock already incurred.

### Why this is the best next action

- Level 11 is the first board where both random play and a plausible one-step strategy fail frequently.
- The problem is not lack of content; it is whether players understand why a locally sensible choice failed.
- A contextual lesson preserves the puzzle's difficulty while improving perceived fairness.
- It is smaller, safer, and more testable than rebalancing multiple levels.
- It directly supports casual, low-confidence, and puzzle-expert segments without weakening mastery.

## Real-user validation plan

Test this recommendation with 10–15 new players in the first closed-alpha wave.

Measure:

- tutorial completion
- first deadlock level
- Undo use after a deadlock
- Level 10 and Level 11 completion
- whether the player can explain why the neighbor rotated
- voluntary continuation to Level 12
- sound disabled within the first session
- qualitative reaction to the first audible effect

Success criteria for retaining the lesson:

- at least 80% of observed players can explain the Level 11 failure after the message
- at least 70% use Undo or another intentional recovery action instead of immediately quitting
- no material increase in confusion for experienced puzzle players
